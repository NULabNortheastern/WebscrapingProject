Using graphs and charts is highly recommended. In fact, replace text with visuals wherever possible. They are a great way to communicate complex information. The use of diagrams and other visuals is a very effective and visually attractive way to illustrate concepts and describe methods. 

You can add many kinds of charts to your presentation: bar charts, trend lines, pie charts, scatter plots, spider charts, and more. However, the graphics and charts should be of high-quality, accurate, and self-explanatory. 

Look at the following example and think which one is better? And why? [Check the font size, color contrast, labels, readability, etc.]

When you make charts, always ask yourself, “How much detail do I need?” Presenters often include too much data in their on-screen charts. Focus on conveying the most important data and information, and choose a chart that best suits your communication needs.

Tables are good for side-by-side comparisons of data.  However, tables can lack impact, so wherever possible use charts for quantitative data. If you choose to use a table do not forget to limit text, make content clear and visible. That said, you should always clearly label your charts and tables.

Avoid using PowerPoint Clip Art from the older versions or other cartoonish line art; instead explore icons in the newer PowerPoint versions. You can also use royalty-free vector images and illustrations from the internet.

Lastly, remember that a single image, illustration, photograph, chart is far more powerful than a slide cluttered with images.
